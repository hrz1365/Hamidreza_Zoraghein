Forecasting COVID-19 and Other Infectious Diseases for Proactive Policy: Artificial Intelligence Can Help (Journal of Public Health 2023):

This paper advocates for leveraging Artificial Intelligence and Deep Learning in modeling and forecasting the spread of infectious diseases. We also explore the symbiotic potential of integrating Deep Learning with conventional epidemiological approaches. Our empirical foundation lies in using LSTM and XGBoost to model daily COVID-19 rates at the U.S. county level. Notably, our weekly forecasts consistently ranked among the top 5 models, as validated by the U.S. COVID-19 forecast hub.

People and infrastructure: multi-scale assessment of coastal and fluvial flood exposure in India (Environmental Research Communications 2023)

This paper evaluates the exposure of urban and rural populations and critical infrastructure of each India's state to both coastal and fluvial floods. It uses a great variety of datasets inclusing different flood layers, census data, high resolution remote sensing products and crowd-sourced data for the geospatial analysis. Our results, presented at the state level, suggest high exposure to fluvial flooding with about 184 million or more than 1:7 of India's population at risk. This proportion is somewhat higher for rural dwellers (15.8%) compared with urban residents (14.2%). Urban residents, however, are much more likely to be affected by coastal floods, likely due to the high population densities of India's coastal megacities. In total, around 19,218 (15%) of health and 34,519 (18%) of educational facilities are exposed to either coastal or fluvial flood risks. A spatially detailed, locally refined, comprehensive flood risk assessment such as this is critical to inform and target public policy and guide disaster risk reduction plans. By improving infrastructure, increasing awareness, and developing proactive, targeted, and inclusive flood plans, communities can build resilience.

Spatiotemporal prediction of COVID-19 cases using inter-and intra-county proxies of human interactions (Nature Communications 2021):

In In this paper, we use human interaction measurements as proxies for predicting COVID-19, a challenging task at high spatial resolutions. We develop a Spatiotemporal autoregressive model, utilizing infection rates, human interactions, mobility, and socioeconomic data to predict county-level new COVID-19 cases in the contiguous US. Human interactions are measured through Facebook and cell phone data, incorporated into separate models. The model is evaluated across 14 forecast dates from October 25, 2020, to January 24, 2021, with prediction horizons of one to four weeks. Comparing with a Baseline model, our model shows an average 6.46% improvement in Mean Absolute Errors (MAE) over the two-week horizon and up to 20.22% improvement over the four-week horizon, indicating strong predictive power in longer horizons.

Population scenarios for U.S. states consistent with shared socioeconomic pathways (Environmental Research Letters 2020):

In this paper, we present the first set of US state population projections covering a wide range of outcomes, driven by state-level demographic rates following the widely-used SSP scenario framework. These projections integrate all 50 states using bilateral migration flows and incorporate updated data and plausible international migration assumptions. The projected national population for 2100 ranges from about 250 million to 650 million, slightly lower than SSP projections due to updated base year data. Population growth varies significantly across states, with the Rocky Mountain region and Utah experiencing the highest increases while the Northeast and Great Lakes regions, along with states like Alaska, California, Louisiana, and Mississippi, seeing slower growth or decline. Aging is evident across all states and scenarios, with the Northeast, Florida, West, and Great Lakes regions showing prominent changes. The relative contributions of fertility, mortality, and migration to population change also differ considerably among states.

A spatial population downscaling model for integrated human-environment analysis in the United States (Demographic Research 2020)

To understand historical demographic patterns and anticipate future changes for environmental interactions, we present a high-resolution gravity-based population downscaling model for each US state. We calibrate the model using historical population grids and evaluate its performance against census-based data from 2010. The model's interpretability and adaptability suggest its potential in depicting state-level spatial population changes, with stable parameters for urban areas but diverse patterns for rural populations.

Data-enriched interpolation for temporally consistent population compositions (GIScience & Remote Sensing 2019): This research evaluates areal interpolation with dasymetric refinement to estimate demographic attributes in Massachusetts from 1990 to 2010. The approach provides consistent estimates within census tract boundaries, enabling nuanced micro-scale analysis of population changes. Various ancillary variables, including GHSL, NLCD, parcels, building footprints, and ZTRAX® dataset, are used to refine the estimates. The results show that this method is effective for longer time spans and larger enumerated populations. Refined TDW using building footprints or ZTRAX® dataset outperforms other methods and improves estimates of urban population. This method offers a promising strategy for creating reliable multi-temporal estimates of population subgroups and informs urbanization and future population projections.

Exposing the urban continuum: implications and cross-comparison from an interdisciplinary perspective (International Journal of Digital earth 2018): The increasing availability of geospatial data on human settlement and population offers new modeling opportunities for characterizing urban, peri-urban, and rural areas. However, little research has been done on the agreement between different data products measuring human presence using various proxy variables. In this study, we quantitatively evaluate and cross-compare data from remote-sensing-based built-up land layers, census-based population estimates, and cadastral data. The assessment utilizes a unique integrated validation database covering different geographic settings in the U.S. We identify the advantages and shortcomings of these data types, providing valuable insights for future data users regarding accuracy and suitability for various applications, even in data-poor regions.

Exploiting temporal information in parcel data to refine small area population estimates (Computers, Environment and Urban Systems 2016): In this study, we analyze small-area demographic data over time using areal interpolation methods to achieve temporal consistency. We utilize cadastral (parcel) data to refine census tracts, aiming for more accurate estimates. The residential land identified through parcel data is used in three interpolation methods: areal weighting (AW), target density weighting (TDW), and pycnophylactic modeling (PM). The analysis focuses on Hennepin County, Minnesota, interpolating population counts from 2000 into 2010 census tract boundaries. Results indicate that parcel-based refinement improves interpolation accuracy, with parcel-refined TDW performing best. Parcel data are particularly effective in rural areas. However, large parcel extents may bias results. The use of parcel-based refinement has the potential to advance long-term demographic analysis where built date attributes are available.
